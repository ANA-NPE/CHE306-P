⚗️
Solvent system — Aqueous MDEA + Sulfolane + H₂O hybrid. MDEA provides selective chemical absorption with low regeneration energy; Sulfolane adds physical absorption capacity at the high CO₂ partial pressure of the feed. Together, this hybrid delivers the high removal performance needed for a 45 mol% CO₂ feed while keeping reboiler duty manageable.
Why this design — by metric
Each card summarizes one outcome of the design study.
🌿
Pipeline-quality sweet gas

The absorber reduces CO₂ from 45 mol% to 2.94 mol% — at or below typical pipeline specifications — and brings H₂S to essentially zero. The result confirms the hybrid solvent's ability to handle a sour, high-CO₂ feed in a single absorption stage.

⚖️
Mass balances close to within 0.06%

Independent hand calculations on a 500 kmol/h basis show molar and mass balance closure errors below 0.06% for both the absorber (T-101) and stripper (T-103), confirming thermodynamic consistency between the simulation and the design model.

🔥
Adiabatic absorber operation

Energy balance shows net duty Q ≈ 0 on T-101. The exothermic CO₂ absorption heat (≈ 6.4 °C solvent temperature rise) is carried out by the rich-amine stream rather than removed by external cooling — consistent with how industrial amine absorbers actually run.

📐
Stage counts match HYSYS exactly

Kremser shortcut analysis (on a solute-free basis) yields ~30 absorber stages and ~25 stripper stages, in exact agreement with the rigorous Aspen HYSYS V14 configuration. This cross-check supports the selected solvent rate and pressures.

📏
~15 m packed-bed height

HTU/NTU analysis based on Sulzer Mellapak 250Y structured packing gives an estimated packed-bed height of approximately 15 m for both columns — a reasonable preliminary value for an MDEA / Sulfolane gas-sweetening service.

🛡️
Safety-aware preliminary design

A layered protection strategy is proposed for H₂S toxicity, CO₂ asphyxiation, methane flammability, wet-H₂S cracking, amine SCC, foaming, and overpressure — with fixed gas detection, sour-service materials, PSV / flare routing, ESD interlocks, and corrosion monitoring. A formal HAZOP is recommended before detailed engineering.

📋
Note on Data — Stream conditions, compositions, and enthalpies were generated in Aspen HYSYS V14 using the Acid Gas — Chemical Solvents fluid package, then scaled to the design basis (500 kmol/h sour gas, 1,893 kmol/h lean amine). All hand-calculation results are checked against the rigorous simulation and against literature correlations for amine sweetening systems.
🔑
Design Basis — Steady-state, pipeline-spec sour-gas treating service. Effective equilibrium slopes (m = 6.945 absorber, m = 12.55 stripper) are back-fitted from the Aspen rigorous VLE so the Kremser shortcut matches the non-linear MDEA / Sulfolane equilibrium. The solute-free Kremser form is used because CO₂ feed concentration is high (45 mol%).
Process flow diagram
Sour gas enters the bottom of the absorber while lean amine enters the top. Rich amine is depressurised, heated, and regenerated in the reboiled stripper; the recovered lean amine is recycled.

Key: Sienna lines = sour / acid-gas streams · Sage = sweetened product · Goldenrod = amine streams. The lean/rich heat exchanger (HX) recovers heat from the regenerated lean amine to pre-heat the rich solvent before it enters the stripper.

⚖️
Mass balance — T-101 absorber

Steady-state, 30 theoretical stages, no accumulation. The rich-amine flow is calculated from the original HYSYS absorption ratio of 0.1445 kmol absorbed per kmol of lean amine.

FSour Gas + FLean Amine = FSweet Gas + FRich Amine
500 + 1,893 = 226.4 + 2,166.6 = 2,393 kmol/h ✓

Closure is exact at the molar level and to within 0.06% on a mass basis — confirming consistency between the design and the simulation.

🔥
Energy balance — T-101 (adiabatic)

Total enthalpy in = total enthalpy out at the new flow rates. Net column duty is essentially zero — the exothermic CO₂ absorption heat (~6.4 °C solvent rise) is carried out by the rich-amine stream rather than removed by external cooling.

ΣHin = −8.839 × 10⁸ kJ/h
ΣHout = −8.839 × 10⁸ kJ/h
Qabs = ΣHout − ΣHin ≈ 0 kJ/h → adiabatic ✓
📐
Kremser equation — absorber stages

Because CO₂ is at high concentration (45 mol%), Kremser is applied on a solute-free basis with mole ratios Y = y/(1−y). The effective equilibrium slope m = 6.945 is back-fitted from Aspen's rigorous VLE.

YN+1 = 0.45 / 0.55 = 0.8182 mol CO₂/mol dry gas
Y1 = 0.0294 / 0.9706 = 0.03029
Vs = 500 × (1 − 0.45) = 275 kmol/h
A = L / (m × Vs) = 1,892 / (6.945 × 275) = 0.9906

Solve Kremser ⇒ N ≈ 30 theoretical stages ✓ (matches HYSYS exactly)

The absorption factor A ≈ 1 indicates the solvent rate is operating just above the theoretical minimum, which maximises driving force per stage but leaves limited margin against operational upsets.

♨️
Kremser equation — stripper stages

The stripper uses an apparent equilibrium slope m = 12.55 reflecting the steep CO₂ desorption curve. To keep the calculation physically valid, xout = 0.00005 (the actual non-rounded Aspen value) is used instead of Aspen's displayed 0.0000.

S = m·V/L = (12.55 × 200) / 2,018 = 1.244
xin/xout = 0.0599 / 0.00005 = 1,198

N = ln[1 + (S − 1)(xin/xout)] / ln(S) − 1
N = ln(293.3) / ln(1.244) − 1 = 5.682 / 0.2183 − 1 = ≈ 25 ✓

S > 1 confirms that the vapour phase is the favoured phase for CO₂ — stripping conditions are correctly driving CO₂ out of the rich solvent.

📏
HTU / NTU — column heights

Using Sulzer Mellapak 250Y structured packing with HETP ≈ 0.50 m/stage (absorber) and 0.60 m/stage (stripper):

Absorber: NTUOG ≈ 30.2, HTUOG ≈ 0.497 m → H ≈ 15.0 m
Stripper: NTUOL ≈ 22.4, HTUOL ≈ 0.671 m → H ≈ 15.0 m

Both columns converge to ~15 m — a reasonable preliminary value for an MDEA / Sulfolane gas-sweetening service. Final design must include flooding, pressure drop, vendor packing data, and a rate-based simulation check.

Safety evaluation & layered protection
High-pressure sour service introduces toxicity, asphyxiation, flammability, corrosion, and process-upset hazards. The design uses a layered protection strategy — inherent safety, BPCS, alarms, SIS, relief, gas detection, and emergency response.
⚠️
Hazardous service — The unit handles 1 mol% H₂S at 5,200 kPa, where leak partial pressure can rapidly produce IDLH atmospheres (NIOSH IDLH = 100 ppm; OSHA ceiling = 20 ppm). Olfactory fatigue makes smell unreliable as a warning. This unit must be treated as H₂S service with fixed gas detection and permit-to-work for all maintenance.
Hazards & engineered safeguards
Each hazard is paired with the recommended preventive and protective layers.
☠️ H₂S toxic release
Leak from absorber, flash drum, stripper overhead, sample point, or rich-amine line
Safeguards: Fixed H₂S detectors (10 ppm low / 20 ppm high alarm) at all leak-credible locations · portable detectors for field work · permit-to-work · escape SCBA · closed sampling · vent routing to a safe flare.
😶‍🌫️ CO₂ asphyxiation
CO₂-rich vent or leak displacing oxygen in enclosed / low-lying areas
Safeguards: Stripper overhead routed to a safe elevated stack with adequate dispersion · oxygen-deficiency monitoring in nearby enclosed areas.
🔥 Methane fire / explosion
Sweet-gas leak mixing with air within the flammable range
Safeguards: Hydrocarbon gas detectors · hazardous-area electrical classification · ignition-source control · grounding & bonding · nitrogen purging at start-up / shutdown.
🧱 Sulfide stress cracking / HIC
Wet H₂S exposure causing hydrogen damage near welds (cf. Union Oil Romeoville rupture, OSHA 1986)
Safeguards: Sour-service material selection · HIC-resistant plate where required · post-weld heat treatment (PWHT) · hardness control · periodic weld inspection.
🧪 Amine stress corrosion cracking
Hot lean amine + residual weld stress (Bagdasarian, Shargay & Coombs 1992)
Safeguards: Stress-relieved welds in lean-amine and stripper service · lean-amine temperature control · inspection of hot-amine circuits.
💧 Erosion-corrosion at rich-amine letdown
Two-phase flashing flow after pressure reduction (Nielsen & Lewis 1995)
Safeguards: Stainless / hard-faced valve trim · velocity control downstream · corrosion monitoring · scheduled inspection of letdown piping.
🫧 Foaming & amine carryover
Hydrocarbon contamination, FeS solids, degradation products, or heat-stable salts
Safeguards: Inlet separation · coalescing filtration · routine solvent analysis · high-DP alarms across packed beds · controlled antifoam injection (root-cause action, not permanent fix).
⬆️ Gas blow-by to flash drum
Loss of absorber bottom liquid level exposing the flash drum to absorber pressure
Safeguards: Low-low level interlock · PSV sized for gas blow-by case · relief routed to a closed flare per API 521 (no atmospheric venting because of H₂S).
🔥 Reboiler overheating
Loss of rich-amine feed flow while steam continues to the reboiler
Safeguards: Fail-closed steam valve · low-low rich-amine flow trip · low-low stripper level trip · high reboiler temperature trip.
🚨 Acid-gas breakthrough
Loss of lean-amine circulation while sour gas continues to enter
Safeguards: Low-low lean-amine flow interlock that closes the sour-gas inlet ESD valve. Treated as safety-related; SIL determined by formal LOPA per IEC 61511.

🎯
Conclusion — The proposed MDEA / Sulfolane absorption unit is technically feasible within the project scope. CO₂ is reduced from 45 mol% to 2.94 mol%, H₂S to essentially zero, mass and energy balances are consistent, Kremser shortcut and HYSYS rigorous results agree on stage count, and HTU/NTU gives a reasonable ~15 m packed bed. With rate-based validation, economic optimisation, and detailed HAZOP/LOPA, the unit can be designed for safe, effective sour natural gas sweetening.
